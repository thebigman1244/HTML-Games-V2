AP Physics 1 — Kinematics

Unit 1 | Chapter 2: Motion in One Dimension
1. Displacement & Distance

Displacement (Δx) is the change in position of an object. It is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.

Δx = xf − xi

Distance is the total path length traveled. It is a scalar quantity (magnitude only).

Example: A student walks 5 m east, then 3 m west. Distance = 8 m, Displacement = +2 m east.
2. Velocity & Speed

Average velocity is displacement divided by elapsed time:

vavg = Δx / Δt

Average speed is total distance divided by elapsed time:

speedavg = total distance / Δt
Key Distinction: Velocity is a vector (includes direction), speed is a scalar. An object returning to its starting point has vavg = 0 but speed > 0.
3. Acceleration

Acceleration (a) is the rate of change of velocity:

a = Δv / Δt = (vf − vi) / Δt

Units: m/s². Positive acceleration means velocity is increasing in the positive direction.

Example: A car accelerates from 10 m/s to 30 m/s in 4 s. a = (30 − 10) / 4 = 5 m/s².
4. Kinematic Equations (Constant Acceleration)

When acceleration is constant, four equations relate displacement, velocity, acceleration, and time:

vf = vi + at
Δx = vit + ½at²
vf^1^ = vi^1^ + 2aΔx
Δx = ½(vi + vf)t
Problem-Solving Strategy:
1. Identify known variables (vi, vf, a, t, Δx).
2. Choose the equation that contains your knowns and one unknown.
3. Solve algebraically before plugging in numbers.
4. Check units and sign conventions.
5. Free Fall

Objects in free fall experience only the acceleration due to gravity:

g = 9.8 m/s² (≈ 10 m/s² for estimation)

Use kinematic equations with a = −g (if up is positive) or a = g (if down is positive).

Example: A ball is thrown upward at 20 m/s. How high does it go?
vf = 0 at peak, a = −9.8 m/s²
vf^1^ = vi^1^ + 2aΔx → 0 = 400 + 2(−9.8)Δx → Δx ≈ 20.4 m
6. Newton's Laws of Motion

First Law (Inertia): An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force.

ΣF = 0 → a = 0 (equilibrium)

Second Law: The net force on an object equals its mass times acceleration:

ΣF = ma

Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. Forces always come in pairs on different objects.

Free-Body Diagram Tips:
1. Isolate the object of interest.
2. Draw all forces acting ON the object (not by the object).
3. Choose coordinate system aligned with motion.
4. Write ΣFx = max and ΣFy = may.
7. Friction

Normal force (FN) is the perpendicular contact force from a surface.

Kinetic friction opposes sliding motion:

fk = μkFN

Static friction prevents motion from starting:

fs ≤ μsFN
Note: μs > μk for the same surfaces. Static friction adjusts up to its maximum value.
8. Practice Problem
Problem: A 5 kg block sits on a horizontal surface (μk = 0.3). A horizontal force of 25 N is applied. Find the acceleration.

Solution:
1. Vertical: ΣFy = 0 → FN = mg = 5(9.8) = 49 N
2. Friction: fk = μkFN = 0.3(49) = 14.7 N
3. Horizontal: ΣFx = Fapplied − fk = 25 − 14.7 = 10.3 N
4. Acceleration: a = ΣF / m = 10.3 / 5 = 2.06 m/s²
